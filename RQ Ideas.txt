THESIS RESEARCH QUESTION IDEAS
(LLM-assisted, deterministic-first threat modeling for LLM-enabled applications)

== Option A — Reference: human expert ==

Main:
To what extent does domain-specific fine-tuning of a local LLM (qwen3:8b) improve the accuracy of its risk classification and the quality of its mitigations, relative to its base version, when evaluated against expert security judgment within a deterministic-first, questionnaire-driven threat-modeling system?

Sub:
A1. To what extent does fine-tuning improve agreement with expert-assigned OWASP risk levels compared to the base model?
A2. To what extent does fine-tuning improve the relevance, correctness, and specificity of mitigations, as rated by experts?
A3. To what extent does fine-tuning reduce false positives (risks experts judge not applicable)?

== Option B — Reference: existing AI threat-modeling tool (e.g., STRIDE GPT) ==

Main:
To what extent does a deterministic-first system with a fine-tuned local LLM produce risk assessments and mitigations comparable to an established LLM-based threat-modeling tool (e.g., STRIDE GPT), and to what extent does fine-tuning narrow the gap relative to the base model?

Sub:
B1. To what extent do the fine-tuned model's risks and mitigations align with those of the reference AI tool (coverage and overlap)?
B2. To what extent does fine-tuning increase this alignment compared to the base model?
B3. To what extent does the deterministic-first design yield higher reproducibility and grounding than the free-form AI tool?
B4. When a strong LLM acts as a blind judge, to what extent is the fine-tuned output preferred over the base output?

== Cross-cutting (applies to both) ==

C1. To what extent does the deterministic-first architecture preserve output reliability and reproducibility regardless of the LLM's quality (base vs fine-tuned)?
C2. To what extent does questionnaire-driven, OWASP-mapped analysis reduce hallucinated risks compared to free-form LLM generation?
